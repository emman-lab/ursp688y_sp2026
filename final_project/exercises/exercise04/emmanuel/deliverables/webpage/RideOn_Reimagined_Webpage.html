Did Ride On Reimagined Phase 1 close or reproduce a transit-service equity gap?

This project responds to an imaginary request from the Montgomery County Department of Transportation to evaluate whether the Ride On Reimagined redesign distributed scheduled bus service gains toward Census tracts with higher estimated transit need.

Problem Statement And Central Question

Bus network redesigns are often framed as technical improvements: routes are adjusted, frequencies are balanced, and schedules are redesigned to create a more efficient system. The equity question is whether those technical improvements also benefit people and neighborhoods that rely most on transit. In Montgomery County, Ride On Reimagined provides a useful case because Phase 1 created a concrete before-and-after schedule change that can be evaluated with public data.

The central question is: did Ride On Reimagined Phase 1 produce larger scheduled service gains in higher-transit-need Census tracts than in lower-transit-need tracts? The hypothesis tested here is empirical rather than normative: higher transit need is expected to be associated with larger scheduled service gains if the redesign prioritized equity in service distribution.

The analysis is an equity audit of scheduled service supply. It does not claim to measure whether riders experienced better trips, because rider experience depends on reliability, span of service, safety, crowding, fares, transfers, and destination access.

Data And Methods

The analysis uses American Community Survey tract data to estimate transit need and General Transit Feed Specification files to measure scheduled Ride On bus service. ACS variables are combined into a tract-level transit need index, with higher values indicating stronger indicators of transit dependence or transportation vulnerability. The GTFS analysis counts scheduled weekday trips and accessible stops within a 0.25-mile tract buffer, allowing the schedule data to be summarized at the same geography as the ACS indicators.

The primary comparison uses the January 2025 GTFS feed as the pre-redesign condition and the June 2025 GTFS feed as the Phase 1 implementation condition. A separate published 2026 schedule comparison is retained only as a sensitivity check because the feed runs into future dates relative to the analysis date of May 7, 2026.

Results

The central result is that Phase 1 did not show a clear pattern of larger scheduled service gains in higher-need tracts. High-need tracts had the highest baseline level of scheduled service, but their average weekday scheduled trip count declined after Phase 1. Moderate-low-need tracts gained the most service on average.

Figure 3. Average weekday scheduled trips by transit need quartile across the January 2025, June 2025, and May 2026 published GTFS feeds.
This figure shows that the high-need quartile started with more scheduled service than the other quartiles, which matters for interpretation because a service loss does not necessarily mean these tracts became the lowest-served places. However, the line for high-need tracts declines after the redesign, while the moderate-low group rises, so the change pattern does not match an equity-prioritized service gain story.

This figure separates the baseline condition from the policy change. This distinction is central to the project: the finding is not that high-need places lacked all service, but that the redesign's incremental benefits were not concentrated there.

Conclusions And Implications

The results do not support the hypothesis that Ride On Reimagined Phase 1 produced larger scheduled service gains in higher-transit-need tracts. The most important implication is not that the redesign was necessarily inequitable in every sense, but that scheduled service gains were not clearly aligned with the places where ACS indicators suggest greater transit dependence. For a planning agency, that finding should trigger a focused equity review rather than a simple pass-or-fail judgment.

This analysis also illustrates both the strength and weakness of a smart city or data science approach. Its strength is reproducibility: public ACS and GTFS data can identify patterns that might otherwise remain hidden inside technical planning documents. Its weakness is that the same data can make transit governance feel more objective than it really is. A tract-level schedule audit cannot know whether a parent can still reach child care, whether a late-shift worker can make a transfer, or whether riders feel safer after the change. Less problematically technocratic transit governance would use this analysis as a targeting tool for public engagement, not as a substitute for that engagement.

Challenges And Limitations

What The Method Handles Well

The method creates a reproducible bridge between demographic indicators and scheduled transit service. It also separates the main implemented Phase 1 comparison from the 2026 published-schedule sensitivity check, which keeps future schedule data from being mistaken for observed outcomes.

What The Method Cannot Fully Resolve

The method does not measure real-time reliability, trip purpose, transfer burden, fare affordability, crowding, safety, or whether riders can reach meaningful destinations. It also depends on tract-level aggregation and a 0.25-mile buffer, which can simplify actual walking conditions and neighborhood boundaries.
